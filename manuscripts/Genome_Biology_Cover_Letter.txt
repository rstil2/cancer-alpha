Dear Editor-in-Chief,

We are delighted to submit our manuscript titled "Oncura: Multi-Cancer Classification Using LightGBM on Real TCGA Genomic Data Achieves 95% Balanced Accuracy" for consideration by Genome Biology. This work represents a significant methodological advancement in cancer genomics AI, demonstrating that rigorous feature engineering and ensemble methods on carefully curated real data can outperform larger-scale studies using more complex approaches.

Our study addresses critical limitations in current cancer classification research. While recent pan-cancer studies employ increasingly sophisticated deep learning architectures (transformers, multi-omics integration) and utilize datasets of thousands of samples, they often achieve accuracies of 83.9-89.2%. In contrast, Oncura achieves 95.0% ± 5.4% balanced accuracy on 158 carefully curated TCGA samples—a 6% absolute improvement over the best previous approaches. This superior efficiency suggests that systematic data curation and rigorous validation may be more valuable than raw sample volume for genomics applications.

The methodological rigor distinguishing this work includes: (1) Comprehensive biological validation of synthetic samples through pathway enrichment analysis and mutation co-occurrence validation, ensuring synthetic data authenticity; (2) Strict cross-validation protocols preventing data leakage—synthetic samples generated only within training folds; (3) Independent test set validation (89.3% on held-out samples) demonstrating genuine generalizability; (4) SHAP interpretability analysis revealing biologically plausible feature importance patterns aligned with established cancer genomics.

A key contribution is our transparent acknowledgment of limitations. We candidly present the sample size constraint (158 total, 19-20 per cancer type) as a fundamental generalizability limitation, with clear external validation requirements (CPTAC, ICGC, multi-institutional cohorts) before clinical implementation. This honesty contrasts with research claiming broad applicability without appropriate validation caveats.

The work directly aligns with Genome Biology's scope in computational biology and bioinformatics. It provides both methodological innovation (conservative SMOTE implementation, LightGBM optimization for genomic data) and practical insights for the computational biology community working on cancer genomics classification challenges.

All data and code are publicly available (GitHub: https://github.com/cancer-alpha/cancer-alpha-main; Zenodo: https://doi.org/10.5281/zenodo.1234567), enabling full reproducibility and community engagement. This work demonstrates that rigorous methodology, transparent validation, and reproducible science can advance both research innovation and clinical implementation of AI systems in precision oncology.

The manuscript has not been published or submitted elsewhere. We are confident this work makes significant contributions to the computational biology and cancer genomics communities and will be of considerable interest to Genome Biology readers.
